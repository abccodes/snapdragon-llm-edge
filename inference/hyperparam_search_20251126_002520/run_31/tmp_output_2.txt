The color of blood is primarily determined by its oxygen content. When blood carries oxygen to tissues throughout the body, it appears bright red due to the presence of hemoglobin, a protein found in red blood cells that binds to oxygen molecules. As blood travels through veins and returns to the heart, it loses some of this oxygen, causing it to appear darker and bluer.
This is because light interacts with the different components of blood differently. Hemoglobin absorbs certain wavelengths of light more strongly than others, which causes the blue coloration. The exact wavelength that is absorbed depends on the type of hemoglobin in the blood and its chemical state. For example, deoxygenated hemoglobin (which has lost some of its oxygen) appears bluer than oxyhemoglobin (which still contains most of its oxygen).
