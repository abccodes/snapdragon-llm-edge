One of the most significant concerns is hypothermia, which can occur quickly in cold temperatures with wet hair. When your body loses heat faster than it can produce it, you may experience shivering, muscle cramps, weakness, confusion, dizziness, and eventually unconsciousness or death if not treated promptly.
Another risk to consider is frostbite, which occurs when the skin and underlying tissues freeze due to prolonged exposure to cold temperatures. Frostbite can cause numbness, pain, redness, swelling, blisters, and in severe cases, tissue damage that may require amputation.
Additionally, wet hair can increase your chances of catching a cold or other respiratory illnesses by allowing viruses and bacteria to enter through the skin and nasal passages more easily. Cold weather also makes it harder for your immune system to fight off infections, so you are more susceptible to getting sick when your body is already compromised due to wet hair.
To avoid these risks, it's important to stay dry and warm in cold weather. If you have wet hair, try drying it thoroughly before going outside or covering it with a hat or scarf
