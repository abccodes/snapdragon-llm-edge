The Sun's appearance in space can be quite different than what we see on Earth. On our planet, the Sun appears as a bright yellow orb, but when observed from space, it takes on a much more interesting hue.
From space, the Sun appears orange-red or even brownish due to its high temperature and radiation. The reason for this is that the light emitted by the Sun is made up of different colors, each with a specific wavelength.
When these wavelengths reach Earth's atmosphere, they interact with various molecules and particles in the air, causing them to scatter or absorb certain colors more than others. This process is known as Rayleigh scattering, which causes shorter wavelengths (such as blue) to be scattered more easily than longer wavelengths (like red).
As a result, when we look at the Sun from Earth's surface, most of the short-wavelength light is absorbed by our atmosphere, leaving behind mostly long-wavelength red and orange hues. This is why the Sun appears yellow or white to us.
